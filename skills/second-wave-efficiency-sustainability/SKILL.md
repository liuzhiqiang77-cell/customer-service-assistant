# The 'Second Wave' Efficiency Strategy

Build on initial turnaround success by launching a "Second Wave" that integrates middle management and achieves sustainable efficiency. This phase addresses the limitations of crisis-mode restructuring that temporarily bypassed middle management layers.

## When to Apply This Skill

Use when:
- Initial successful turnaround ('hot-wiring' phase) is complete
- Momentum and energy from early changes are waning
- External threats loom (e.g., market deregulation)
- Need major improvements in efficiency for long-term survival
- Initial frontline empowerment lacks "viable alternatives to old middle management roles"
- Organization faces mature turnaround rather than acute crisis

## Diagnosis: Why the Second Wave is Necessary

### The Limitation of Crisis-Mode Turnaround

The initial approach of "short-circuiting" middle management:
- Successfully empowered frontline for immediate crisis response
- Bypassed middle managers to speed decision-making
- **Problem:** Does not form the base for sustained vitality
- **Root Cause:** Lacked "viable alternatives to their old role as rule interpreters"

### The Sustainability Gap

Crisis mode works for survival but creates:
- Middle managers with unclear purpose
- Temporary structures not designed for longevity
- Efficiency gains that may plateau
- Organization unprepared for long-term market volatility

## Second Wave Objectives

1. **Achieve major improvements in efficiency** - Beyond initial crisis gains
2. **Prepare for impending market changes** - e.g., European airline deregulation
3. **Integrate middle management** - Redirect them toward serving the market
4. **Create sustainable structure** - Flatten the pyramid permanently
5. **Establish efficiency as "people issue"** - Through and through, not just physical assets

## Execution Steps

### Step 1: Acknowledge the Transition

- Recognize that crisis-mode flattening was phase one
- Accept that middle management needs reintegration, not elimination
- Prepare for deeper structural change

### Step 2: Flatten the Pyramid Permanently

- The "distribution of roles is radically different"
- Make structural changes permanent, not temporary
- Ensure new roles are viable and sustainable

### Step 3: Integrate Middle Management

**Ignite Middle Management:**
- Bring middle managers back into the process
- Redirect them toward serving the market
- Replace "rule interpreter" role with "market facilitator" role

**New Middle Management Purpose:**
- Support frontline empowerment
- Solve systemic problems
- Remove barriers to efficiency
- Connect operational realities to strategic goals

### Step 4: Emphasize Efficiency as a People Issue

**Shift Focus:**
- FROM: Physical assets, equipment, technology
- TO: People, processes, organizational design

**Key Insight:**
- "Efficiency is a people issue, through and through"
- Sustainable efficiency comes from empowered, engaged people
- Technology supports, but doesn't replace, human initiative

### Step 5: Prepare for Market Changes

- Use efficiency gains as buffer against external threats
- Build organizational flexibility
- Enable rapid adaptation to deregulation or competition

## The Evolution of Organizational Structure

### Phase 1: Crisis Turnaround (Hot-Wiring)
- Rapid bypass of middle management
- Direct top-to-frontline communication
- Frontline autonomy without clear middle manager roles
- **Result:** Quick survival, temporary structure

### Phase 2: Second Wave (Sustainability)
- Middle management reintegrated with new purpose
- Permanent pyramid flattening
- All roles aligned with market service
- **Result:** Sustainable efficiency, long-term vitality

## Variables and Context

| Variable | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Market Driver | External threat necessitating Second Wave | European airline deregulation |
| Initial State | Success of Phase 1 turnaround | Crisis resolved, frontline empowered |
| Gap | Missing middle manager role definition | No viable alternative to "rule interpreter" |
| Goal | Sustainable organizational structure | Flattened, efficient, market-responsive |

## Success Indicators

- Middle managers actively serving market needs
- Efficiency improvements beyond initial crisis gains
- Organization prepared for market deregulation or competition
- Clear, viable roles for all organizational levels
- Structure designed for long-term, not temporary survival
- "People issue" orientation to efficiency
- Sustainable vitality rather than crisis-driven energy

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Impact | Solution |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Never launching Second Wave | Momentum wanes, middle management disengaged | Recognize need for Phase 2 integration |
| Reverting old middle management roles | Rule-interpretation returns, bottlenecks reemerge | Create new viable roles focused on market |
| Focusing only on physical/asset efficiency | Limited gains, people disengage | Treat efficiency as "people issue through and through" |
| Making flattening temporary rather than permanent | Structure reverts to hierarchy | Permanently change role distribution |
| Ignoring external market threats | Unprepared for deregulation/competition | Use efficiency to build market readiness |
